Evaluation of the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum’s Online Collection

Lucy Doyle, Spring 2026

Part 1: Background

Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum: A Brief Background

The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum in Boston, Massachusetts is not a typical fine art museum. The museum’s architecture, collection, and organization was guided by one woman, Isabella Stewart Gardner. Isabella was born into a wealthy family in New York City in 1840. In 1860, she married John “Jack” Gardner, whose family was well-known and well-respected in Boston. After the death of their only child, Isabella and Jack travelled throughout Europe and Asia, returning frequently to Venice. Jack and Isabella were inspired by the art and culture they encountered on their travels and dreamed of opening a museum in Boston in order to expose the public to the art they encountered abroad (Dykstra, 2024, p. 3). With the help of Bernard Berenson, an art collector Isabella met while studying art history at Harvard, Isabella began building her collection of art and rare books (“Meet Isabella”). After Jack Gardner died unexpectedly in 1898, Isabella began planning in earnest for the museum that they had dreamed of together. As the museum was built in the Fenway neighborhood of Boston, Isabella was closely involved with every part of the process. “Fenway Court,” as the museum was known until Isabella’s death in 1924, featured an inner courtyard insulated with a glass roof and a fourth-floor apartment for Isabella. Each floor looked onto the courtyard, and the architecture within the building was meant to invoke the palazzi of Venice, despite Fenway Court’s somewhat drab brick exterior walls (Dykstra, 2024, p.277). Isabella collected and commissioned much of the architectural details of the building abroad, ensuring that each inch of the building aligned with her vision.

Once the building was finished in 1901, Isabella organized each gallery herself. At this point, Isabella had amassed a large, varied collection of paintings, sketches, sculptures, furniture, and more. She wanted each gallery to be positioned in such a way that it would engage and delight patrons. She did not organize by time period, originating region, or any other metric typically used by an art museum. She was trying to highlight the facets and jewels of her collection, while recreating the feelings of excitement and discovery she experienced in her travels abroad (Dykstra, 2024, pp. 274-275). This unique, individual organization is fundamental to the museum. In her will, Isabella left enough money to support the museum, but stipulated that the organization of the galleries could not be changed (Dykstra, 2024, p. 387). Thus, visitors of today who enter the original galleries of the museum are experiencing the collection just as Isabella intended, the same as the visitors who saw the collection upon the museum’s opening in 1903. The Gardner opened a new wing in 2012 housing a performance hall, new exhibition galleries, and spaces for conservation and maintenance of the collection (Waldheim, 2022). This addition enabled the museum to keep up with the interests of 21st museum-goers while continuing to take care of and feature Isabella’s original collection in the way that she intended.

The Gardner’s Online Collection

The museum’s website includes a searchable online collection. The home page for this online collection has a number of featured items, including some of the museum’s most famous paintings, a search bar, and a set of filters. The filters allow users to sort the collection based on room (where the piece is housed), century, country, category (such as architectural detail, furniture, painting, etc.), or artist. All of the filters except for the “Artist” category have a drop down list that the user can scroll through to make their selection. The “Artist” filter lists the artists in alphabetical order by last name, but has its own mini search function which makes the extremely long list more manageable to navigate. Users are able to stack filter choices, allowing them to search for “paintings from Italy in the blue room” using just a combination of filter selections. Once a user conducts a search via the search bar or the filters, they are given a list of search returns. Within the search return, each item has a thumbnail with an image (if one exists), the artist’s name or the region of origin, the title of the piece, and a year.

Selecting an item from the list of search returns brings you to that item’s object entry page. This page lists all of the information the museum has about that item. The object entry page includes a digitized version of the item or photos of it. Most of the paintings and furniture objects include a photo of the object in the context of the room it is displayed in. The images below come from the object entry page of John Singer Sargent’s painting “San Giuseppe di Castello, Venice”. One image shows just the painting, while the other shows it in context on the wall of the Blue room.

The page may include an object description. The following object details are listed as well:

  • Object number
  • Creator(s)
  • Title
  • Date
  • Medium
  • Dimensions
  • Signatures, inscriptions, or markings
  • Provenance
  • Commentary
  • Bibliography
  • Gallery location
  • Rights and reproductions notes

There is a button enabling the easy export of the object details. The creator name(s) are hyperlinked. Clicking on them leads the user to a pre-filtered search retrieval displaying all of the works in the collection from that creator. At the bottom of the object page, there is an indication of whether the object is on view or not, and a link to explore the room that it is displayed in. Clicking on the link brings the user to a page overviewing that gallery room.

The gallery room descriptions are not technically a part of the “Online Collection” information system, but they are linked to within the page of every object that is on view, and are key to the online collection’s attempts to emulate the physical experience of visiting the Gardner Museum. Each gallery room page has images of the room, access to an audio tour of the room, and a brief description of the room and its place in the progression of the museum. The page also has a selection of images of the art pieces that are in that room. Clicking on any of these images leads the user to that object’s entry in the online collection. The gallery room page links to a “Room Guide,” a numbered map of the layout of each wall of the room.

The map identifies which piece of art is located where, but does not link to the object pages of these pieces within the online collection. At the bottom of the gallery room page, there are links to the overall museum map and “Rooms Nearby,” further explaining the positioning of the museum for the online visitor.

Part 2: Evaluation of the Gardner’s Online System

While completing my evaluation of the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum’s online collection, I kept the unique history and organization of the museum in mind. Through my research and my experiences as a visitor to the museum, I know that the collection’s organization is fundamental to the mission of the museum. Any online version of the museum’s collection must engage with this organization, or it would be missing a key part of the experience of the collection. How can an online collection emulate the specific physical experience of a collection such as the Isabella Steward Gardner’s?

Overall, the Gardner’s online collection appropriately highlights the unique physical characteristics of the museum’s collection, but could further take advantage of the online medium to bridge the gap between the experiences of an online visitor and an in-person visitor. The online collection design is visually consistent and engaging, while not clashing or distracting from the images of artwork. The aesthetic is appealing and appropriate. The filters are easy to use. The inclusion of “Room” as a filter option shows that the developers were aware of the importance of the collection’s physical positioning. This filter, along with the metadata identifying which room each object is in, is a smart way to allow the online user to get a virtual sense of the physical positioning of the collection. Additionally, online users are able to explore a narrower slice of the collection with more ease than in-person visitors because of the ability to use multiple filters at once within the online collection’s search retrieval functioning. An online user can easily select “paintings,” “John Singer Sargent,” and “Blue Room” through the filters, resulting in a more targeted cultivation than an in-person visitor is capable of. This ability to explore the online collection broadly and narrowly, while still maintaining awareness of the positioning of the objects, is extremely useful both for researchers and casual online users and consistent with the in-person experience of the Gardner’s collection.

While the search retrieval functions have many useful facets, there are some areas where they and the object entry pages they lead to fall short. Within the search function, the order of retrieved items is unclear and unchangeable by the user. The amount of information in an object entry page ranges, with some having extensive photos and explanations of how the piece came to be and others having no photos and a generic commentary note, pictured below.

Given the general commentary note, it is likely that the object entry pages are inconsistent because more is known about some objects versus others, and users are being given as much information as possible. This echoes the “More Product, Less Process” movement that has gained popularity with archivists in recent years. The “MPLP” approach argues that it is better to prioritize user access to materials in a collection rather than processing them to a certain standard before allowing user access (Greene & Meissner, 2005). While this does enable users to discover objects sooner, lack of context can create confusion. This being said, the Gardner Museum’s online collection items always specify whether the object is on view and where it is located. That this metadata is considered essential to each object further shows how the online collection fundamentally embraces the physical positioning of the collection as key information, even in an online setting.

The creation and interlinking of gallery room pages are effective at illustrating the physical characteristics of the museum to the online user. The design of each gallery room page evokes that specific room without overwhelming the user. Each gallery room page uses images, text, and audio to offer the online user a multi-sensory, immersive experience. Since the gallery page offers easy paths to the museum map and specifies which other rooms are nearby, online users can naturally approximate the experience of walking through the museum in-person. The “Room Guide,” a wall-by-wall map in each gallery room page is the only feature that leaves online users wanting. The map of each wall is numbered and refers to a key of bibliographic information for each piece of art identified. If a user is intrigued by a specific object in this room guide, they need to copy and paste this bibliographic information into the online collection to pull up the object entry page on their own. This discourages users from using the room wall guide as a springboard to further engage with the artwork within the online collection.

Part 3: Recommendations

The Gardner Museum’s online collection is robust and fundamentally mirrors the unique physical experience of visiting the museum, which is key to the Gardner’s mission to continue the vision of Isabella Stewart Gardner. The following recommendations suggest some areas where further links could be made between existing resources to bolster the online visitor’s experience. I’ve listed them in order of priority.

First, the Room Guides should directly link to the object entry page of each piece of art. This would support the online user’s ability to browse the collection as if they were standing in the room. At the moment, the Room Guides are just digitized versions of the brochures and visual aids given to in-person visitors. It would be a better use of the online medium if the virtual version of these materials linked directly to the object entry page in the online collection. This would also encourage engagement with the online collection from casual online visitors not searching for information about a specific piece. The work required to link each piece in each Room Guides would not be wasted time, since Isabella Stewart Gardner specified that the gallery rooms could not be reorganized or changed. Linking each bibliographic record with the appropriate object entry page would be time consuming but ultimately it would enhance the online user’s experience and increase engagement with the individual object entry pages in the online collection.

Second, it would be useful to have individual artist pages within the online collection. At the moment, each entry in the “Creator” field in the object entry page is hyperlinked, but that link leads to a pre-filtered search retrieval for just the works associated with that creator. It would be useful if those links instead brought the user to a page with biographic information about that artist and a brief explanation of how their work ended up at the Gardner Museum, in addition to the object entry pages of their other works in the online collection. This would further highlight the unique acquisition history of the pieces at the Gardner. It would also provide additional avenues for the online visitor to discover the artistic importance of many of the works at the Gardner within the overall body of work of artists such as Sandro Botticelli, Rembrandt, Raphael, Titian, and John Singer Sargent. A lot of this information already is recorded in the commentary notes for various objects, but organization into artist pages would offer another avenue of exploration to the online user and highlight the history and importance of the Gardner’s collection.

Third, there are some minor adjustments that could be made to the search retrieval interface. Once a user conducts any type of search, they are provided with a list of retrieved search results. The user is given no explanation as to the order of these retrieved search results and is not able to select any type of order for them. This dilutes the usefulness of the filters that are provided. If an online user uses just the room filter, they are provided with over a thousand results, given the density of the artwork in each gallery room. The user could then further refine their search using the other filters, but they may just click through a few pages of results then lose interest, overwhelmed by the number of items. If the user was able to order the results or understand the pre-existing order, they may stick around for longer engaging with the collection.

Conclusion

The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum's online collection appropriately approximates the aspects of what make the museum and its collection unique. The museum is the vision of a single woman, and it is difficult for an online system to capture the specificity of her decisions regarding the physical space of the Gardner. Developers of the online collection built that physical awareness into the system with their inclusion of a “Room” filter and its subsequent metadata. This is also achieved through the linking of each object entry page to the relevant gallery room page. There are opportunities to further this work, however, by enhancing the gallery room pages so that they link back to the object entry pages more comprehensively. The addition of artist pages in the online collection would be another avenue for leveraging the online medium to showcase the Gardner's unique history of acquisition and organization. The static nature of much of the Gardner’s collection offers an opportunity, as work would not need to be redone as objects move as they would in a more traditional fine art museum. While there are areas for improvement, the Gardner’s online collection successfully allows an online visitor to engage with the collection in a manner that is in line with Isabella Stewart Gardner’s intentions and the museum’s mission overall.
