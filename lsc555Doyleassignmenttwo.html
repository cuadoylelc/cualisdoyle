Heuristic Evaluation

Overall, the system is visually pleasing and clear. The design is not cluttered, and there is no excessive unnecessary information. Necessary information, like headlines, bylines, publish dates, and sections the story is included in (i.e. "Feature," "News," "Quill [the editorial section]"), is clearly identified and displayed consistently for the user. This makes it extremely easy for the user to identify who wrote the story, when it was published, and what sections it was included in. These are all pieces of information that would be fundamental for a professor or student to be able to identify while searching for relevant material. Additionally, once you are reading a particular article, the navigation design of the page clearly shows how you can trace your steps back to an earlier page. For my test, I chose the topic of "student metro use." I wanted to see how students wrote about their and their peers' use of and attitudes towards the DC metro system over time. I think this would be an interesting topic for a CUA professor to assign, challenging their students to think about how they interact with the rest of the city and what that interaction has looked like over time on CUA’s campus. Through this test topic, I concluded that the system returns accurate results, but does not allow for much flexibility or customization to a user’s topics of interest.

My search term of "student metro" provided many useful search results. However, the ranking system for the returned results was unclear. The results were not ordered by date, as the first page offered articles ranging from 2016-2026. It looks like the results are most likely ordered by relevance based on frequency of the key terms. I was not able to filter the results in any way. As a student researching a narrow topic, or a professor searching for a representative article, it would be extremely helpful to filter the results by date published. Filtering by section published to would be useful as well. Perhaps a professor is not interested in editorials, and wants fact-checked articles, while a student who is researching student attitudes toward metro use may just want to see editorials. The ability to filter in some way would make research on the site more precise and easier to tailor to the user’s specific topic. Additionally, a researcher would have to rely on appropriately choosing key words for their search in order to find useful resources. Users are quite limited in the ways that they can browse the site. They can choose a section such as "arts & entertainment," "politics," or "sports," look at content by month (i.e. September 2019, March 2020), or perform a key word search. Once an article of interest from a key word search has been opened, there are suggestions of "More Stories" that appear below the article. These suggestions are the same no matter which article you are reading, and are not thematically or temporally related to the open article at all. If the suggested articles were related in any way to the open article, user browsing would be encouraged, and alternative information of interest may be found. As it works now, browsers and specific researchers are limited by the key words that they use to perform a search.

Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths

  1. Clear design with fundamental information consistently displayed.
  2. Quick and Error-free.

This system’s strengths lie mainly in its display aesthetic. The design is minimalistic, but always includes fundamental, key information such as the story headline, date published, author, and section published to. This information is clearly displayed within search results and once you open a specific article This uniformity is exemplified by the screenshots below. Almost all of the stories are accompanied by a picture, which keeps the design streamlined but still engaging. A handful of older stories are not accompanied by a picture, but they are few and far between. This key information is consistently displayed in the same locations, making it extremely easy for the user to find. Additionally, the website menu is always at the top of the page, making it easy for the user to access. The menu is not cluttered with possibilities, and is streamlined to basic key locations. This consistency builds trust with the user, encouraging them to believe that the information they are finding is well-reviewed and accurate. Consistency with fundamental information also implies stability and reliability, which are adjectives that news organizations generally want to be associated with. Given that it is particularly important for a news organization to encourage users to trust and rely on them, the focus on fundamental information is a smart use of space within the minimalistic design.

This system continues to build trust by focusing on the fundamentals via its speed and lack of errors. The system is simple, with a handful of sections and no advanced search function, but it is quick and effective at achieving basic functions. I ran into no errors during my investigation. Every button worked and led to information that was relevant to the button label. Nothing was misleading or unclear. This may be basic, but for a student newspaper site that has likely been edited and maintained by countless individuals over the years, this consistent lack of errors encourages user feelings of trust, reliability, and stability, much like the website’s overall design aesthetic.

Weaknesses

  1. Limited to exactly what you see.
  2. Limited avenues for browsing.

As discussed above, the system is fairly simple, and simplicity with accuracy has its benefits. However, as a user of this system, you are limited to exactly the information that you see. The "about" section of the page clearly identifies the newspaper’s current staff, including their names, hometowns, contact information, staff position, and a photo. It does not, however, have any information about the paper’s history. It does not point the user towards alternate places that may have this information. It lists staff information and staff information only. If a user is looking for any other information about the paper, they receive no indication of where they might be able to find that information. Similarly, there is an "Archives" sidebar that has hyperlinks to each month’s content from April 2026 back to September 2015, in chronological order. While it is useful to have that sidebar clearly identified and easily accessible, there is no indication for how a user may access content older than September 2015. This would be frustrating for any user seeking such information. It would be helpful if the system acknowledged these gaps in information and provided guidance to users for where to look next.

As I discussed in my heuristic evaluation, once a user begins browsing the site beyond a surface level, they quickly realize that they have just three possible ways to browse. They can choose a section from the website menu, choose a month from the "Archives" sidebar, or conduct a key word search. There is no way to filter the website or their search by date or section published to. The suggested stories that populate when a specific article is being read are not related to the open article at all, and remain the same no matter which article you open. This is consistent, so it does not cause confusion for the user, but the limited browsing ability does hamper a user who does not know exactly what they are searching for. If a professor or student is browsing for articles that pertain to a specific topic or time period, they are unlikely to come across them naturally. They can look at a specific newspaper section, articles published within a specific month, or articles retrieved based on a keyword search, but they cannot combine any two of these buckets to browse at once and come across something useful for their purposes.

Reflection and Improvements Based on a Mini-Usability Test

The results of the mini-usability test mainly illustrated the weaknesses I found in the system. The participant acknowledged that the system was "attractive and easy to figure out" and made use of two of the three browsing avenues that I identified (key word searching and choosing a month from the "Archives" sidebar). The surface level aesthetic aspects of the website are strong, but those strengths were quickly bypassed during the mini-usability study. Since the participant was motivated by specific tasks, she was not particularly impressed with the aesthetic effectiveness of the system. She said that, once she was attempting her tasks, the system was "straightforward" but "wasn’t actually very helpful." This is very much in line with the weaknesses in browsing and further information access that I identified. The participant ran into a wall pretty quickly, and became extremely frustrated by the inability of the system to adjust at all to make the information she was searching for easier to find. This is a logical continuation of the impact of the system limits that I identified have on users who are searching for information related to a specific topic. It is good that the system did not devolve into confusion or contradiction when a user was trying to find something that the system could not easily provide, but the fact that the system could not accommodate such a basic search is ultimately a failure. Additionally, as predicted by the weakness I identified of being limited to just what is clearly displayed on the website, once the participant found reference to a potentially helpful article, she ran into a broken hyperlink with no information as to where to go next. The broken links, paired with the lack of guidance on where to look for information closely related to the Tower, cause immense frustration and encourage users to abandon the site in favor of a google search. This is a tremendous weakness that likely will discourage most users attempting to use the site for anything beyond surface level browsing.

In order to better support professors and students searching for primary source materials, I think the system should add filters to its search return functions. Once a user conducts a search, they should be able to choose whether results will be presented to them by relevance or in chronological order of publish date. They should also be able to see which section of the newspaper the returned articles were published to, and be able to filter the results based on this information. These additional features would make use of metadata that already exists in the system, and would not require changes to the basic keyword search mechanism that is already in place. They would allow the user to customize their searches to produce a more useful batch of search returns. This increases the likelihood of a professor or student coming across an article that is related to their topic and useful, without having to know that that article exists or when/where it was published to. Additionally, it is imperative that the broken links be fixed or, at the very least, lead to an error page that acknowledges the dead end and offers the user conclusive information as to where to look next.
